                    Does phytosterol have side effects?<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/h2>

                    Phytosterols can cause <\/span>a range of side effects.<\/span><\/b> Occasionally, they lead to symptoms such as nausea and skin rashes. They also impair the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins<\/a> and carotenoids. <\/span>For this reason, fortified foods and supplements are not suitable for young children or pregnant and breastfeeding women.<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/p>

                    In addition, there are indications that a high phytosterol intake may promote cardiovascular disease. Although phytosterols help lower cholesterol, their structure is similar to that of cholesterol. As a result, they may also build up in blood vessels and promote the development of\u202f <\/span>atherosclerosis<\/a>.<\/span>6<\/sup><\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/p>

                    Impaired phytosterol uptake due to genes<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/h2>

                    Rare mutations in the ABCG5 and ABCG8 genes can lead to excessive absorption of phytosterols. Anyone affected by this genetic condition, known as phytosterolemia, should <\/span>strictly limit their intake of phytosterols<\/span><\/b>.<\/span>7<\/sup><\/span><\/p>

                    Phytosterole in creams<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/h2>

                    Rare mutations in the ABCG5 and ABCG8 genes can lead to <\/span>increased uptake<\/span><\/b> of phytosterols<\/span>. <\/span>As an ingredient in creams, for example, they are believed to help improve skin elasticity and moisture. <\/span>S<\/span> Studies have shown that they can support skin repair, among other benefits<\/span>.<\/span>8<\/sup><\/span> The use of phytosterols in care products is considered safe. <\/span>They typically do not cause adverse effects such as skin irritation.<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/p>
