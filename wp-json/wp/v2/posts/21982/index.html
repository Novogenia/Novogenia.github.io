                                      Can <\/span>you<\/span> ingest<\/span> too<\/span> much<\/span> riboflavin<\/span> with<\/span> food<\/span>?<\/span><\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/h3>

                                      An overdose with normal foods is <\/span>practically impossible<\/span><\/b>. According to current knowledge, riboflavin does not cause any side effects even if you take a multiple of the normal daily requirement as a dietary supplement. The body simply excretes the excess with the urine.<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/p>

                                      • So far, the EFSA, the authority responsible for food safety in the EU, has therefore refrained from setting a maximum daily dose<\/span>.<\/span>2<\/sup><\/span><\/span><\/span><\/li><\/ul>

                                          Who needs a vitamin B2 supplement?<\/h2>

                                          Healthy people usually get enough riboflavin from their diet. In this case, the additional use of a vitamin B2 supplement is not necessary.\u00a0<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/p>

                                          The situation is different for members of a <\/span>risk group<\/span><\/b> . For them, the additional intake as a dietary supplement can be useful to ensure a needs-based supply or to compensate for an existing riboflavin deficiency.<\/span>\u00a0<\/span><\/p>\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t
